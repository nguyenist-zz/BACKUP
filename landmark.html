LANDMARK DIRECTIONS

Visual navigation without the maps.

ABOUT LANDMARK

Landmark is a mobile application for walking directions with step-by-step photos of geotagged landmarks. While most navigation applications are rooted in maps, a significant percentage of people prefer navigating visually, based on landmarks.

We're leveraging open source data (GPS, routing, points of interest) to create an app that allows you to get from point A to point B using photos & language specifically catered to pedestrian (4 blocks instead of 0.8 miles, Head toward Starbucks instead of Go NW). Our application and other platforms brings back the human element of intuitive directions. See more on our website here.

Logo and branding process

Landmark is a new way of getting on point A to point B without relying on compasses and paper maps. We wanted the logo to reflect a cleaner, simpler way of navigating. While incorporating a famous landmark in the logo seemed intuitive at first, many users immediately thought the app involved adventure and tourism in a new city instead of an alternative way of getting your everyday directions. After dozens of iterations, we decided to go with a fresh blue and red toned compass needle with fresh, modern typography. It emulated the simplicity of Landmark.

Wireframing and paper prototyping.

We have focused on gathering helpful photos & designing UX that is more useful to some users than existing applications. We conducted a series of tests to understand what is the fastest way to gather photos of a city through Taskrabbits, Craigslist interns, photo taking in Lyft Cabs, gamification. Computer vision (CV) was the most efficient method. So, we developed a dashboard for data interns to manually select landmark photos from Foursquare across 10 cities. After categorizing thousands of photos with humans, we can use CV to sort more photos for us, much more quickly, at scale. 5 weeks and 80+ walking tests later, we have refined both our design and our method and are testing each week.

Over several weeks, we went on simulated routes and tweaked the prototype daily, designing with user feedback and interactions from the previous day. We narrowed it down to 2 designs that users found intuitive and enjoyed. We conducted more simulated walking routes, timed them and determined which design allowed users to orient and navigate easily and effectively. In total, we have interviewed or walked with over 300 people to validate.
